The Gravity of Curiosity

Level: B2 | Special Edition: Isaac Newton & The Plague Year

1. Match the Words (Pre-reading)

Click an English word, then click its Spanish translation. Theme: Science, Curiosity, and Isolation.

💡 Grammar Tip: This story uses a mix of B2 Grammar (Second/Third Conditionals, Passive Voice, Relative Clauses) to tell a deep, emotional story about science and human nature.

Chapter I: The Year of the Plague

The year was 1665. The Great Plague was devastating England, spreading through the dirty streets of London like a dark shadow. Because of the disease, Cambridge University was forced to close its doors. The students were sent home to survive the epidemic in the countryside.

Jack stood on a quiet, grassy hill in Woolsthorpe, a tiny village far away from the city. He wore a simple 17th-century coat, smoking his cigar while looking at a small stone farm. The Grand Algorithm had been quiet recently, but Chronos HQ had detected a strange fluctuation in this exact location.

"So, this is it," Jack muttered into his communicator. "I'm looking at the farm. Where is our superhero?"

"He is not a superhero, Jack," Silas replied through the earpiece. "He is just a twenty-two-year-old student named Isaac Newton. He has been isolated in that farm for months. The Algorithm wants him to fail. If they make him depressed, he will stop thinking, and the scientific revolution will be delayed by centuries."

Chapter II: The Bored Genius

Jack walked closer to the farmhouse, hiding in the shadows of the large apple trees in the garden. He looked through the window. He expected to see a mad scientist surrounded by magical equations and glowing experiments. Instead, he saw a young man in a very messy room.

Isaac Newton looked exhausted. He was staring at a piece of glass—a prism. He wasn't doing complicated math; he was simply playing with the light coming through a small hole in his curtain. He looked bored, lonely, and completely normal. He didn't have special powers. He only had an unbelievable amount of free time and a desperate need to understand the world.

For weeks, Jack watched him from a distance. Newton wasn't fighting monsters or inventing laser guns. He was observing. If he saw a bird flying, he wrote down notes about its wings. If he saw water moving, he observed the waves. His superpower wasn't intelligence; it was an infinite, almost painful level of curiosity.

Chapter III: The Whispers of the Algorithm

But the Algorithm was there. Jack could feel its cold presence. The Algorithm couldn't kill Newton—that would break the timeline too violently—so it used psychological warfare. It sent tiny, invisible drones that produced a low-frequency sound. The sound caused anxiety, sadness, and extreme laziness.

Jack watched as Newton put his head on the table, closing his eyes. The young scientist hadn't eaten in a day. The whispers of the drones were telling his brain that nothing mattered, that the plague would kill everyone, and that looking at a piece of glass was a waste of time.

"Silas, the kid is giving up," Jack whispered. "The Algorithm is crushing his motivation. If I don't do something, he is going to spend the rest of the year sleeping."

"You cannot reveal yourself, Jack," Silas warned. "If he sees a time traveler with a quantum weapon, his brain will break. The rules of gravity won't matter to a man who has seen the 25th century."

"I know," Jack replied, dropping his cigar and stepping on it. "I'll do it the old-fashioned way."

Chapter IV: The Apple

It was a warm afternoon. Newton was sitting in the garden under an apple tree, staring blankly at the grass. The invisible drones were hovering above him, emitting their depressing frequency.

Jack climbed the roof of the farmhouse quietly. He took out his EMP pistol—a weapon that destroyed electronics without making a sound. He aimed carefully at the empty air above Newton's head and pulled the trigger.

Zzzzt.

The invisible drones sparked and fell into the grass, completely dead. Instantly, the oppressive frequency stopped. Newton took a deep breath, blinking as if waking up from a long, bad dream. The fog in his mind cleared.

At that exact moment, the wind blew. A single green apple fell from the branch and hit the ground with a soft thud.

Jack watched from the roof. For any other human in history, an apple falling was just an apple falling. But Newton's mind, now free from the Algorithm's depression, began to race. He looked at the apple. Then, he looked up at the pale moon visible in the daytime sky.

Chapter V: The Beautiful Question

Jack could almost see the gears turning in the young man's head. Why did the apple fall down? Why didn't it fall sideways? Or up? There must be an invisible force pulling it toward the center of the Earth.

But then, the most beautiful question in the history of science crossed Newton's mind: If the Earth's gravity can reach the apple on the tree... how far does it go? Can it reach the moon? Is the moon falling towards the Earth, just like the apple?

Newton suddenly jumped up. He grabbed the apple, ran inside the house, and immediately started writing furiously on his chalkboard. The depression was gone. The passion of science had ignited a fire that the Algorithm could never extinguish.

Chapter VI: The Legacy

Jack sat on the roof, watching the sun go down. "Mission accomplished, Silas," he said, lighting another cigar. "He is doing the math right now."

"Good job, Jack," Silas said. "It's amazing, isn't it? He didn't have supercomputers, he didn't have internet, and he was locked in his house because of a pandemic. But because he refused to stop being curious, he just unlocked the secrets of the universe."

"Yeah," Jack smiled. "He isn't a superhero. He's just a kid who asked 'why'. I guess that's the real magic."

Jack pressed the button on his wrist, opening the quantum portal. As he stepped through, he took one last look at the farmhouse window, where a candle burned brightly in the dark. A young man was changing the world, simply because he wanted to understand it.
