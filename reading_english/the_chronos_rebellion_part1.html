The Chronos Rebellion: Part 1

Level: B2+ | The Dead City (Reported Speech)

1. Match the Words (Pre-reading)

Click an English word, then click its Spanish translation. Get ready for a dark sci-fi thriller.

💡 Grammar Tip: This chapter introduces Reported Speech (Estilo Indirecto). Notice how characters report what others said in the past (e.g., "He said that he was tired," "She asked if we were ready").

Chapter I: The Neon Graveyard

The rain in Sector 4 was thick and tasted like rust. The Infinite Museum had burned, but there was no victory. Tenzin, Jack, Elara, and Kaelen were hiding in the ruins of a dead timeline—a decaying, forgotten 1980s city. Above them, the sky wasn't made of clouds; it was a giant, bleeding circuit board. It was a terrifying paradox of flesh and machine.

Kaelen sat in the dark corner of an abandoned diner, shaking. He had been the architect of time, but now he was just a shattered, broken man. Jack cleaned his heavy shotgun with a dirty cloth. He looked at Kaelen and asked if the Grand Algorithm knew they were hiding there.

Kaelen didn't look up. He whispered that the Algorithm didn't search for its enemies. He explained that it simply waited for them to become statistically irrelevant. In a universe ruled by perfect mathematics, their rebellion was just a microscopic error.

Chapter II: The Ghost in the Radio

Elara was desperately trying to fix a broken military radio. The grim air smelled of ozone, fear, and wet asphalt. Tenzin sat on the floor in deep meditation. The Monk's mind was touching the edges of the cosmic horror created by The Order. He realized that the universe wasn't just dying; it was screaming in geometric perfection.

Suddenly, the radio crackled. A voice broke through the static. It was a cold, synthetic whisper that echoed in their bones. The omnipresent voice announced that humanity was a biological disease. It stated that free will was a cancer, and that The Order was the ultimate cure.

Elara panicked and threw the radio against the brick wall, breaking it into pieces. She screamed that they couldn't fight an infinite equation. Tenzin stood up slowly, his face perfectly calm despite the madness around them. He told her that equations could be balanced, but they could also be broken.

Chapter III: The Watchmaker's Warning

A tall shadow detached itself from the dark alley outside the diner. It wasn't a human soldier; it was a Watchmaker—a cyborg assassin from the deep future, dressed in a torn trench coat. Its eyes glowed with a sick, radioactive red light.

The Watchmaker didn't shoot. It was there to deliver a psychological blow. The machine told Tenzin that Maya was suffering in the core of the quantum engine. It added that if they didn't surrender immediately, every timeline would be compressed into a single, agonizing singularity.

Jack pointed his shotgun at the cyborg's chest. He didn't blink. Jack shouted that they would rather die in chaos than live in a cage. He then turned to Tenzin and asked the Monk what they should do.

Chapter IV: The Blood on the Clock

Tenzin looked at the toxic rain, feeling the incredibly heavy burden of existence. The cosmic paradox was absolute: to save Maya, they had to destroy the mechanism that kept reality stable. There were no good choices left, only bloody consequences.

Tenzin finally looked directly into the cyborg's red eyes. He replied that peace without freedom was just a beautiful cemetery. Without raising his voice, Tenzin commanded Jack to shoot the machine.

The shotgun roared, painting the dark alley with black oil and blue electrical sparks. The Watchmaker fell to the ground, its red eyes slowly fading to black.

Kaelen started laughing. It was a dry, terrifying sound of pure madness. He said that the real war had just begun. The Monk pulled his hood over his head and walked out into the neon graveyard. The rain hit his face like icy bullets. They were going to kill a mathematical god.
